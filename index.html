Research

Most mammalian genes have multiple isoforms which are generated through the use of alternative transcription initiation sites, termination sites, and internal exons. High-throughput sequencing technologies enabled the discovery and quantification of many novel RNA species including protein-coding RNAs, microRNAs, long non-coding RNAs and others. The most widely used sequencing technologies generate reads that are short relative to the length of the transcripts. Thus, computational methods are needed to reconstruct transcripts and infer their expression levels from the RNA-seq data. I am developing such tools that let us understand gene regulatory processes in health and disease. I am a believer in open and reproducible science, and I always try to follow best practices in my work.

About Me

My name is Foivos (pronounced: Fivos) and I come from the beatiful island of Corfu, Greece. During the last 5 years I live and work in Basel, Switzerland where I obtained my PhD in Bioinformatics.

I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at the lab of Professor Mihaela Zavolan, University of Basel, Switzerland working on computational methods to study RNA-processing in health and disease. I am developing software and pipelines to analyze data ranging from RNA-seq, poly(A)-seq and ribo-seq to single cells and third generation sequencing technologies (PacBio, Oxford Nanopore). At the same time, I am collaborating with experimental biologists to help them with the analysis and interpretation of their data.

Apart from a Ph.D. in Bioinformatics, I hold an MSc in Bioinformatics from the National and Kapodistrian University of Athens, Greece (Department of Biology) where during my thesis I worked on computational methods for the identification of Molecular Recognition Features (MoRFs) in membrane proteins. I also hold a diploma (5 year's course) in Electronics and Computer Engineering from the Techincal University of Crete, Greece. During my thesis, I applied machine learning methods on a breast cancer microarray dataset.
